## Actionable structure patterns

### 1) Title angle patterns that recur
- `Best <product> under $200 (<year>)`
- `<N> Best ...` listicle count in title
- Proof/credibility tokens: `tested`, `reviewed`, `updated`, `expert picks`
- Price qualifier appears early (`under $200`, `budget`, `best value`)

**Template action:** lead with exact keyword + price qualifier in first 60 chars, add current year + proof token.

### 2) Section order pattern (dominant)
1. Hook + why grinder matters (short)
2. Quick picks / editor’s choice callout
3. Comparison table (above fold)
4. Individual grinder reviews (ranked slots)
5. Buying guide (burr type, grind range, retention, noise)
6. FAQ
7. Short conclusion + affiliate disclosure

**Template action:** lock this order; place comparison table before long-form product sections.

### 3) Comparison table style pattern
Most successful pages provide a scan-friendly table with:
- Product
- Best for
- Price (or price band)
- Burr type (conical/flat)
- Grind settings count
- Capacity
- Espresso-capable (yes/no)

**Template action:** add a strict “espresso capable” and “price checked” signal to table schema.

### 4) Product block pattern
Common repeat structure:
- One-line verdict
- Pros / cons bullets
- Key specs
- Explicit “best for” buyer fit
- Sometimes a “why trust this pick” note

**Template action:** require a buyer-fit sentence and one hard differentiator (e.g., settings count, retention behavior, serviceability).

### 5) FAQ / PAA pattern seen repeatedly
Recurring questions:
- Are burr grinders really better than blade grinders?
- Is a $200 grinder good enough for espresso?
- How long should a burr grinder last?
- How often should I clean the grinder?
- Is Baratza Encore still worth it vs newer options?

**Template action:** FAQ should target espresso viability, durability, maintenance cadence, and value tradeoffs.

## Intent gaps competitors leave open (ranking opportunity)

1. **Weak budget-tier decision support**
   - Many pages mention “under $200” but don’t segment `$0–99`, `$100–149`, `$150–200` with clear winner picks.

2. **Espresso capability is often vague**
   - Competitors rarely label whether a grinder is truly espresso-capable out of the box vs “can do fine grind but not ideal.”

3. **Low maintenance clarity**
   - Cleanup effort, static/retention, and clog risk are not consistently scored.

4. **Freshness vs stale list problem**
   - Several ranking pages are old or lightly updated; they keep legacy picks without “price checked this month” credibility.
