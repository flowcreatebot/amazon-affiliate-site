## 2) Actionable structure patterns extracted

### A) Title angle patterns that consistently rank

- `Best Manual Coffee Grinders in <year>` (high authority list style)
- `Top N under $100` + year (budget-first framing)
- “No BS / Tested / From Our Hands” proof language beats generic “buyer guide” phrasing.
- Strong titles combine **budget ceiling + use case** (travel, espresso, pour-over) instead of only “best manual grinder”.

### B) Typical section order on ranking pages

Observed order across top listicles:
1. Short positioning intro (who this guide is for)
2. **Top picks quick list** (often with “overall / budget / espresso / travel” labels)
3. Detailed grinder cards (verdict + specs + fit)
4. “How we chose / testing criteria” trust block
5. Buying guide (burr type, adjuster style, speed/effort, capacity)
6. FAQ block (4–8 Qs)

What underperforms: thin pages with only a mini table + short FAQ and no per-product fit language.

### C) Comparison table style patterns

Most useful table fields across competitors:
- Product
- Price or price band
- Burr material/type
- Adjustment system (internal vs external; stepped granularity)
- Capacity (g)
- Best for / use case
- Espresso suitability (yes / limited / no)

Notable finding: top pages convert by mixing **spec + workflow columns** (e.g., “time/effort”, “dial-in ease”) rather than only technical specs.

### D) FAQ / PAA patterns

Recurring FAQ intent in winners:
- Are manual grinders better than electric under $100?
- Ceramic vs steel burrs?
- Can sub-$100 manual grinders do espresso?
- How long does hand grinding take?
- How to clean and maintain?
- Internal vs external adjustment differences?

Community/PAA overlap from Reddit results strongly reinforces “espresso realism under $100” and “which model gives the least frustration”.

### E) Intent gaps we can exploit

1. **Espresso-subintent gap**: SERP has clear demand for “under $100 + espresso capable”, but many pages are generic/manual-only.
2. **Decision-speed gap**: many long listicles; few give a clear decision tree (“buy this if / skip this if” at budget level).
3. **Workflow realism gap**: limited practical guidance on hand-grinding effort, dose time, and dial-in pain.
