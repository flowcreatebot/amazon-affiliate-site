# Style Guide: VS Comparison

## Purpose & Reader Intent

The reader is choosing between two specific products or categories. They want a clear recommendation and the reasoning behind it. They have already narrowed their options and need help picking.

## Word Range

**Target: 1,200–1,600 words**

Two products compared across 3-5 features, plus verdict, buyer profiles, and FAQ. The HeroVersus component and comparison table carry the factual load, so prose should focus on opinion and nuance.

## Structure Rhythm

1. **Lead** (2-3 sentences) — frame the reader's actual dilemma, not the product category
2. **Quick verdict** (2-3 sentences) — name the winner and the exception
3. **Comparison table** — factual comparison, no prose wrapper
4. **Feature comparisons** (1-2 paragraphs each, min 3 features) — opinion and nuance only
5. **Buyer profiles** (1 paragraph each) — "Choose X if... Skip X if..."
6. **Supporting gear** — related product cards if relevant
7. **FAQ** (min 3 items, 2-4 sentences each)

## Section-Specific Guidance

### Lead Paragraph

**Density:** 2-3 sentences
**What belongs here:** The reader's motivation for comparing these two things. Frame it as a decision, not a topic.
**What to cut:** Product history, category background, feature lists.

**Good example** from `aeropress-vs-french-press`:
> If you are hunting for the best AeroPress alternative, you are probably not looking for a random gadget. You want a brewer that fixes something specific. Maybe you want a cup with more body. Maybe you want less plastic. Maybe you just want something that still feels simple before your brain turns on.

**Why this works:** Addresses the reader's actual motivation, not the products' specs.

### Quick Verdict

**Density:** 2-3 sentences
**What belongs here:** Clear recommendation + the fork in the road (when the other option wins instead).
**What to cut:** "It depends" without a specific condition. Trying to please both sides equally.

### Feature Comparisons

**Density:** 1-2 paragraphs per feature
**What belongs here:** Opinion, sensory description, and context that the comparison table cannot convey. Analogies that help the reader understand the difference.
**What to cut:** Restating data from the comparison table. More than 2 paragraphs on any single feature.

### Buyer Profiles

**Density:** 1 paragraph each (one for each product)
**What belongs here:** "Choose X if you value A and B. Skip X if you need C."
**What to cut:** Restating feature comparison conclusions.

### Fork in the Road

**Pattern to use:** When neither main option fits, explicitly guide readers elsewhere. "If you want X instead of both, look at Z."

**Good example** from `aeropress-vs-french-press`:
> If you are moving away from AeroPress because you want less plastic or a fuller mouthfeel, French Press is the best replacement. If you are moving away because you want cleaner flavor and more ritual, go pour-over instead.

**Why this works:** Catches readers who would otherwise bounce because neither option fits.

## Pre-Submission Checklist

- [ ] Comparison table carries all factual data; prose adds opinion only
- [ ] Each feature section is 1-2 paragraphs, not 3+
- [ ] Clear winner named in verdict (not "it depends" without conditions)
- [ ] Fork-in-the-road pattern used when neither option fits some readers
- [ ] Word count is within 1,200-1,600
